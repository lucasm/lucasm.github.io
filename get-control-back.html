Get Control Back

You are here. Your data is out there. When immersed in the digital world, we are bombarded by attempts to change our behavior, excessive surveillance or even data breaches. This is a guide about taking back control.

1. Web Browser and Search Engine

All starts when you open your browser and search on the Web. But if the company behind them sells advertising every step your do is tracked, and your data collected for third-party use. You can switch. Use a secure browser and search engine that respect your privacy by default, non-profit.

Firefox DuckDuckGo Tor

2. Cookies, Ads and Trackers

Cookies are really good for personalized browsing experiences, and you can chose to able or disable. But ads everywhere? There is no control. To do this, web trackers collect your data to segment specific ads to you. In worse cases, some of they are used to create a “fingerprint” to indentify you based on your combinated browser and device information or, even, inject malware. Just block them with a extension.

uBlock Origin AmIUnique

3. Network, DNS and VPN

Your Internet Service Provider (ISP), or anyone else listening your Internet connection, can see every site you visit and every app you use — even if their content is encrypted. Creepily, some providers sell data about your Internet activity or use it to target you with ads, or even worse, violate the principle of net neutrality. You can solve this using an alternative more secure DNS (Domain Name System) resolver. You can also use a VPN (Virtual Private Network) that mask your IP address and protect your traffic from external monitoring.

1.1.1.1 Hotspot Shield

4. Accounts Data and Passwords

You can use browser default password manager or another to generate and store your passwords securely, and apply two-factor authentication wherever you can. But your password is not safe if it can be brute-forced or found in a database of leaked passwords, right? And if any account of yours has already been compromised in a data leak? Having trouble deleting your account from a service? You can check all of these.

5. Social Media, Chat and Email

Periodically review your privacy settings on social media. Don't use social logins. Period.

Signal ProtonMail

6. File Sharing and Metadata

You probably use a cloud storage service, and that's good, especially if have encryption capabilities. However, sometimes you want to temporary share files with privacy, and your can't. And when you share photos on social media or files over the Internet, you ends up exposing himself sending metadata that identifies you, like timestamp, location and device type info — in fragile democracies, if you participate in popular protests and share these photos online, you can be identified or even persecuted. You can get around all this.

Firefox Send Metadata2Go Image Scrubber

7. OS and Apps Permissions

You can enable automatic updates to keep your operating system and applications up to date. But your activity history, personal files, location, camera, microphone, inking and typing can be tracked by OS and apps. It's strongly recommended that you check the “privacy” section in your OS settings, to disable running apps in the background and enforce restrictions to permissions, both in your desktop and mobile device. If you want complete control over your data and settings, and or total privacy, choose between one of the free, secure and open source Linux distributions to use on your desktop.
